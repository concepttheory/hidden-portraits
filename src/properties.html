sex

male

The Hidden Portraits deal with paintings from other epochs, which had a completely different set of values than we have today. These include the unchallenged supremacy of men.

Portraits of men are mainly an expression of wealth combined with power. Whether inherited or acquired, the male portraits breathe the self-assured attitude that the Hidden Portraits frame and satirize in many ways.

female

In portraits of women over the centuries, one notices the basic decorative mood in which they were created. As time passed there were gradual improvements. Women were eventually depicted with insignias of power (by marriage), or if they were considered intelligent, they might hold books in their hands.

However self-confident, wealthy or smart, their role in society was secondary to that of men. Often the view of women was reduced to a showcase of beauty.

Medium

painting

The historical portraits that serve as the basis of Hidden Portraits are painted reality on two dimensions. Paint and brushstrokes depict skin, body, clothing, and space so that we recognize them as real. The Hidden Portraits modify this painted reality and pay careful attention to the idiosyncrasies of the painters. Each of these magnificent artists had unique characteristics and individual skills, making this amazing art still impressive to this day.

sculpture

If a historic sculpture is the basis of a Hidden Portrait, the starting point is very different. In this case, I am modifying a photograph of a real, three-dimensional object, and unlike paintings, our eyes can register the smallest errors. Sculptures create great technical challenges for me because the collage should be plausible and comprehensible.

drawing

A characteristic feature of drawings is not only the line drawn but rather the lines not drawn. A line can show an outline of a face, the surface we complete in our imagination (because actually, it is the blank sheet of paper). This is particularly challenging for the Hidden Portraits, which must work with these specific elements of drawings.

Gaze

direct

A direct look at the audience also means always making contact. This can be arrogant, challenging or endearing.

indirect

The indirect look, half-hidden, also hides half of the intentions and is more mysterious. Here much is left to our imagination.

hidden

The hidden gaze is rather confusing for us because we are blocked in our estimation of facial expressions. However, the hidden gaze also leaves room for a new interpretation.

features

hair

Hairstyles are sometimes short-lived fashions. Historically some styles were in vogue among a small elite and only very briefly. Because sometimes one's own hair was not full enough, huge powdered and ceremonial wigs were invented to display power and station. As part of the head, they are elementary for the Hidden Portraits. They refer to past fashions and social codes, of which we no longer have any clue.

flowers

Flowers are, of course, a beautiful accessory in portraits. Real or fabric flowers were often worn by women as part of clothing. The hidden portraits frequently use flowers to exaggerate notions of beauty or to draw attention to the role of women in previous periods. But beyond that, there are many established codes of what flowers represent in paintings. They are important metaphors for the interpretation of portraits and an essential component of Hidden Portraits.

jewels

Jewels are rare, precious, and technically difficult to work with. In sum, the perfect tool for representation to this day. For painters they were always a challenge because of their shiny surface and partial transparency. In Hidden Portraits, jewels are an important means of exaggeration. A duplication of jewels in the Hidden Portraits is a well-functioning metaphor for an important rank, decadence, or vanity.

lace

Lace is the goddess among fabrics. Lace has always attracted people -its transparency, delicate nature and difficulty to manufacture have made it a fabric of the elite. It appears in many portraits as a sign of wealth and is important for the Hidden Portraits because it can cover a face and yet allows a mysterious glimpse of the gaze.

fabric

From shimmering silk and complex brocades to elaborate trimmings, human creativity has focussed on the design and decoration of precious fabrics. For the painters of portraits, it has always been a technical challenge to depict the texture of the fabric in such a way that their preciousness is apparent to the viewer. The Hidden Portraits use these fabrics specifically for veiling, pointing out that sometimes fabrics that seem normal to us today were the height of decadence in earlier times.

ribbons

Ribbons and bows are typical mechanisms to make a garment look even more complex. There were instances when an elite sitter was adorned with more than a few ribbons. The painters must have gone mad painting all these small details. For the Hidden Portraits, ribbons are an important instrument of expressing movement. Wrapped widely around the body, they make many a stiff portrait swing.

ruff

What began as a small frill at the neckline evolved into an independent circular collar that could reach enormous proportions. The larger the ruff, the more important the wearer. Located close to the face the ruff is of enormous importance in the Hidden Portraits. Hardly any other fashion accessory can be duplicated in such an absurd yet comprehensible way to hide the face.

armor

The suits of armor we know from many portraits are mostly decorative and ceremonial. Technically they are functional, but mostly they were used to show power and importance through fine craftsmanship. Worn exclusively by men, they are well qualified to make statements about a masculine self-image and attitude in the Hidden Portraits.

fur

Anything hard to acquire becomes expensive, exclusive, and desired by the wealthy. During certain periods only noble people were allowed to wear fur at all. What a sign of rank! The hidden portraits always like to use furs for covering. Since it is a natural material, it becomes a particularly effective alienation.

leather

While leather was a common material for smaller accessories like shoes and belts, in the 16th-century men's jerkins were typically very extravagant leather creations, often slashed and punched, both for decoration and to improve the fit.

century

16th

The age of the Renaissance. The Middle Ages are overcome, the achievements of the ancient world are rediscovered, and the human being returns to the center of thought - all of which are expressed in many historical portraits.

17th

The era of the Baroque. Floating forms, insane excesses and the will to shape everything through the power of the human being. Whether it is nature in geometric gardens or the people themselves in clothes that give the body through huge hoop skirts or high wigs a completely new silhouette.

18th

It starts with the crazy final phase of the baroque, the Rococo, where everyone goes crazy once again. Then the art develops into classicism, again the ancient world is used as a model. Rationality and a more romantic relationship with nature emerge, until everything evolves into Romanticism. All this also had a great impact on the art of portraiture.

19th

A century full of political changes, contradictions and different movements of art. Romanticism, Biedermaier, Realism. This gives rise to a series of different forms of portraits.
